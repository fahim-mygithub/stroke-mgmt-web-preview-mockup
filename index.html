Start Triaging

When a stroke is suspected, first assess ABCT's and determine the next step based on stability.

Evaluate each of the following before selecting an outcome:

  • Airway: Talking normally? Edema / blood / vomitus / foreign body?
  • Breathing: O2 sat < 90%, respiratory rate < 10 or > 30, breath sounds, chest movement, work of breathing, accessory respiratory muscle use
  • Circulation: BP, HR, AMS, cool extremities, skin discoloration, diaphoresis, weak peripheral pulses, arrhythmia
  • Time of Stroke Onset: Last Known Normal
If ABC's are stable: maintain SBP 140–185 mmHg, maintain HR 60–100, and check GCS before proceeding.

Secure airway before continuing any stroke workup.

  • Position, suction aggressively, remove foreign body
  • Consider intubation if GCS < 8 or gag absent
  • Pre-oxygenate; avoid hypoxemia and hypotension

Stabilize breathing before proceeding.

  • Supplemental O2 for SpO2 < 94%
  • Assist ventilation if RR < 10 or > 30
  • Check chest rise and breath sounds bilaterally

Restore perfusion while preserving cerebral pressure.

  • IV access ×2, isotonic fluids if hypotensive
  • Correct arrhythmia per ACLS; avoid over-correction of hypertension
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring

Protect airway early. Low GCS suggests impending loss of protective reflexes.

  • Consider definitive airway
  • Elevate head of bed to 30° if no spinal precautions
  • Recheck pupils, motor response, and glucose

ABCs stable — proceed to scale-based triage.

  • Maintain SBP 140–185 mmHg
  • Maintain HR 60–100
  • Document GCS and proceed to FAST-ED

Stroke Causes and Risk Factors

Dr. Lodi·5 min read·Updated Oct 2023

It's important to distinguish between causes of stroke and modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors. A stroke is caused by an interruption of blood flow to the brain — either from a blocked vessel (ischemic) or a bleed (hemorrhagic). Identifying the underlying mechanism changes the acute treatment pathway.

Ischemic stroke is the most common type, accounting for roughly 87% of cases. It occurs when a blood vessel supplying the brain becomes blocked — most often by a clot that traveled from elsewhere in the body, or by local narrowing of a cerebral vessel.

Non-modifiable risk factors

  • Age — risk doubles every decade after 55
  • Sex — men have higher incidence; women have higher mortality
  • Family history of stroke or TIA
  • Prior stroke or TIA

Modifiable risk factors

  • Hypertension — the single most important modifiable factor
  • Atrial fibrillation
  • Diabetes and metabolic syndrome
  • Tobacco use and heavy alcohol intake
  • Hyperlipidemia and sedentary lifestyle
Distinguishing modifiable from non-modifiable factors is essential for both primary prevention counselling and long-term secondary prevention planning after a stroke.

Takeaways

For clinicians, the priority is rapid identification and treatment. For prevention, the focus should be on aggressive control of blood pressure, management of atrial fibrillation, and lifestyle modification. See related articles for detailed treatment pathways and secondary-prevention frameworks.
